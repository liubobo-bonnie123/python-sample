The Rabbi's Widow Joke

When the Rabbi of Boiberik died, his young widow was so disconsolate that everyone decided she must remarry. So small was Boiberik that the only eligible bachelor was the town butcher. After being married to a scholarly rabbi, marriage to a butcher was a comedown, but the widow, in her loneliness, nonetheless agreed.

The first Friday after the marriage, upon returning from the mikvah, the former rebbetzin was preparing to light the candles when her new husband leaned over to say, “My mother, Hannah, of blessed memory, used to say that after the mikvah and before lighting the candles, it is life-affirming to make love.” And so they did.

After the evening meal, the butcher says to his wife, “My father, Shmuel, an honorable man, instructed me that making love is a fine way of greeting the sabbath.” So off they went to the bedroom.

The next morning, upon waking, the butcher says to his new wife, “My grandmother, Rivka, a sweet soul, always claimed that it is sensible to make love before going to synagogue, the better to clear your mind for prayer.” And so they do.

Upon their return home, settled on their couch, the butcher says to his wife: “My grandfather, Moishe, not a learned but a faultlessly devout man, held that making love after a morning of prayer is the best of all

possible ways to begin the new week.” And so once again they repair to the bedroom.

The next day, at market, the former rebbetzin meets her friend Tema-Gittel, who

asks, “So how is the new husband?” “Well,” replies the former rebbetzin, “a scholar he isn’t, but he comes from a wonderful family.”
